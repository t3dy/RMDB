Referenced primary texts from ancient, medieval, and Renaissance traditions.

Ancient

Asclepius

Hermes Trismegistus • 3rd century CE • Latin Hermetic

A Latin Hermetic dialogue describing the animation of temple statues and prophesying the decline and restoration of Egyptian religion. The 'Lament of the Asclepius' became a key text for Renaissance magi who read it as describing legitimate theurgic operations.

Corpus Hermeticum

Hermes Trismegistus • 2nd-3rd century CE • Greek Hermetic

The foundational collection of Greco-Egyptian philosophical dialogues attributed to Hermes Trismegistus. Ficino's 1463 Latin translation made these texts available to the Renaissance and launched the Hermetic revival. Renaissance readers believed them to be the oldest philosophical texts in existence, predating Moses.

De Mysteriis

Iamblichus • c. 300 CE • Greek Neoplatonic

Iamblichus's philosophical defense of theurgy against Porphyry's objections. Established the principle that the soul cannot ascend through intellect alone but requires ritual operations using material symbols, divine names, and sacred tokens.

Elements of Theology

Proclus • c. 460 CE • Greek Neoplatonic

Proclus's systematic axiomatization of Neoplatonic metaphysics into 211 propositions. Its hierarchical angelology and doctrine of divine names provided key resources for Renaissance Kabbalistic-Neoplatonic synthesis.

Emerald Tablet

Anonymous • uncertain (pre-9th century) • Latin/Arabic Hermetic

A brief alchemical text of uncertain origin, attributed to Hermes Trismegistus, that encodes the doctrine of macrocosm-microcosm correspondence in lapidary phrases. Its maxim 'As above, so below' became the fundamental axiom of Renaissance magical philosophy.

Enneads

Plotinus • c. 270 CE • Greek Neoplatonic

Plotinus's collected philosophical writings, organized by Porphyry into six groups of nine. Ficino's Latin translation (1492) made the full Neoplatonic metaphysical system — the One, Intellect, Soul, Matter — available to Renaissance readers.

Pimander

Hermes Trismegistus • 2nd century CE • Greek Hermetic

The first treatise of the Corpus Hermeticum, describing a visionary cosmogony in which the divine Mind (Nous) reveals the creation of the world. Ficino translated it first under the title Pimander, and it became the most widely read Hermetic text in the Renaissance.

Sefer Yetzirah

Anonymous • 3rd-6th century CE • Hebrew Kabbalistic

The earliest extant text of Jewish mystical cosmology, describing creation through the thirty-two paths of wisdom (ten sefirot and twenty-two Hebrew letters). A foundational source for both Jewish Kabbalah and Renaissance Christian Cabala.

Timaeus

Plato • c. 360 BCE • Greek Neoplatonic

Plato's cosmological dialogue describing the demiurge's fashioning of the world-soul. The single most influential ancient text for Renaissance magical thought after the Hermetica, providing the framework of a living, mathematically structured cosmos.

Medieval

Ars Magna

Ramon Llull • 1305 • Latin Magical

Ramon Llull's combinatory system for generating all possible propositions from a finite set of fundamental concepts. Originally designed as a missionary tool for proving Christian truths to Muslims, the Ars Magna was transformed by Giordano Bruno into a Hermetic memory art, making it one of the most creatively repurposed intellectual systems in Renaissance philosophy.

Ars Notoria

Anonymous • 13th century • Latin Magical

A medieval Latin text prescribing methods for obtaining divine knowledge through angelic communication, combining prayer, fasting, and contemplation of sacred figures. Influenced Renaissance ceremonial magic traditions.

De Docta Ignorantia

Anonymous • 1440 • Latin Philosophical

Nicholas of Cusa's treatise arguing that true wisdom consists in recognizing the limits of rational knowledge and that the infinite God transcends all finite categories. His coincidentia oppositorum influenced Pico's syncretism and Bruno's cosmology.

De Radiis Stellarum

Al-Kindi • 9th century • Arabic Astrological

Al-Kindi's treatise theorizing that all things emit rays capable of producing effects at a distance. Provided the most sophisticated philosophical framework for astrological magic available to Latin Europe and profoundly influenced Ficino's natural magic.

Liber de Causis

Anonymous • 9th century • Latin/Arabic Neoplatonic

A Neoplatonic work falsely attributed to Aristotle, actually derived from Proclus's Elements of Theology. Its doctrine that all things proceed from and return to a First Cause was widely read in medieval and Renaissance philosophy as Aristotelian confirmation of emanation theory.

Opus Majus

Anonymous • 1267 • Latin Philosophical

Roger Bacon's encyclopedic treatise advocating for scientia experimentalis alongside mathematical and linguistic studies. Its discussions of optics, the power of words, and natural magic anticipated the empirical dimension of Renaissance magical philosophy.

Picatrix

Anonymous • 10th-11th century • Arabic Magical

An Arabic compendium of astrological magic, talismanic art, and ritual procedures translated into Latin in 1256. The most comprehensive medieval manual of astral magic, it influenced Renaissance magical practice while remaining officially condemned.

Speculum Astronomiae

Albertus Magnus • c. 1260 • Latin Astrological

Attributed to Albertus Magnus, this treatise distinguishes between licit astronomical observation, acceptable astrological prediction, and condemnable necromantic magic. Its taxonomy framed how Renaissance magi argued for the legitimacy of their practices.

Zohar

Anonymous • c. 1280 • Aramaic/Hebrew Kabbalistic

The central text of medieval Jewish mysticism, a mystical commentary on the Torah composed in Aramaic. Its elaborate sefirot theology, doctrine of divine emanation, and symbolic hermeneutics provided the richest source material for Renaissance Christian Cabala.

Renaissance

900 Conclusions

Giovanni Pico della Mirandola • 1486 • Latin Kabbalistic

Pico's 900 theses drawn from every available philosophical and theological tradition, presented for public disputation in Rome. Thirteen were condemned as heretical, but the project established the principle of universal philosophical concordance.

A True and Faithful Relation

Anonymous • 1659 (composed 1581-1589) • English/Latin Enochian

Casaubon's 1659 publication of Dee's angel diaries, framed as evidence of demonic deception. Despite the hostile editorial framing, this publication preserved the Enochian material and made it available to subsequent practitioners and scholars.

De Arte Cabalistica

Johannes Reuchlin • 1517 • Latin Kabbalistic

Reuchlin's mature Kabbalistic treatise, presented as a dialogue between a Pythagorean, a Muslim, and a Cabalist. The most systematic Renaissance treatment of Kabbalistic methods (gematria, notarikon, temurah) applied to Christian theology.

De Occulta Philosophia

Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa • 1531-1533 • Latin Magical

Agrippa's comprehensive three-book synthesis of natural, celestial, and ceremonial magic. The most influential Renaissance magical encyclopedia, it organized the entire tradition into a systematic philosophical framework and remained the standard reference for over a century.

De Umbris Idearum

Giordano Bruno • 1582 • Latin Magical

Bruno's treatise on the art of memory, using magical images as mnemonic devices that simultaneously serve as instruments of Hermetic contemplation. Transforms Llull's combinatory art into a magical memory system.

De Vanitate Scientiarum

Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa • 1530 • Latin Magical

Agrippa's apparent recantation of the entire magical enterprise, published one year before De Occulta Philosophia. Whether this represents genuine disillusionment, self-protection against heresy charges, or dialectical strategy remains debated.

De Verbo Mirifico

Johannes Reuchlin • 1494 • Latin Kabbalistic

Reuchlin's first Kabbalistic treatise, demonstrating that the divine name Pentagrammaton (YHSVH) encodes the mystery of the Incarnation. Established the method of deriving Christian theological truths from Hebrew letter mysticism.

De Vita (Three Books on Life)

Marsilio Ficino • 1489 • Latin Hermetic

Ficino's practical application of Neoplatonic cosmology to astrological medicine. Book III's instructions for drawing down planetary influences through talismans, music, and fragrance represent Renaissance natural magic's most careful balancing act between philosophy and practice.

De la Causa, Principio et Uno

Giordano Bruno • 1584 • Italian Hermetic

Bruno's Italian dialogue arguing that God and Nature are one infinite substance — a pantheistic monism that pushed Neoplatonic emanation theory to its radical conclusion. Part of his explosive London dialogues of 1583-1585.

Five Books of Mystery

John Dee • 1581-1583 • Latin/English Enochian

Dee's meticulous diary records of his angel conversations with Edward Kelley, covering the earliest phase of the Enochian revelations including the Heptarchic system of angelic governance and the Sigillum Dei Aemeth.

Heptaplus

Giovanni Pico della Mirandola • 1489 • Latin Kabbalistic

Pico's sevenfold allegorical commentary on Genesis, applying Kabbalistic and Neoplatonic methods to the creation narrative. A key demonstration of how Christian Cabala could serve as a tool of scriptural exegesis.

Monas Hieroglyphica

John Dee • 1564 • Latin Hermetic

Dee's dense symbolic treatise proposing a single hieroglyphic sign that encodes all cosmic knowledge. Marks his transition from mathematical humanism to Hermetic philosophy and contains his most concentrated philosophical vision.

Oration on the Dignity of Man

Giovanni Pico della Mirandola • 1486 • Latin Kabbalistic

Pico's declaration of human intellectual freedom, composed as a preface to his 900 Conclusions. Its vision of humanity standing at the center of creation, free to ascend or descend, has become one of the defining texts of Renaissance humanism.

Polygraphia

Johannes Trithemius • 1518 • Latin Magical

Trithemius's posthumous treatise on polyalphabetic ciphers — the first printed book on cryptography. A companion to the Steganographia, it presents the technical cryptographic content without the angelic framework.

Steganographia

Johannes Trithemius • c. 1499 (pub. 1606) • Latin Magical

Trithemius's angel magic treatise that embeds genuine cryptographic ciphers within a framework of planetary spirit communications. Whether the angelic apparatus conceals the ciphers or the ciphers serve genuine spiritual practice remains one of the great puzzles of Renaissance intellectual history.

Theologia Platonica

Marsilio Ficino • 1482 • Latin Neoplatonic

Ficino's magnum opus arguing that Platonic philosophy confirms and enriches Christian doctrine. Its systematic defense of the soul's immortality through Neoplatonic metaphysics established the philosophical framework within which all subsequent Renaissance magical philosophy operated.

Utriusque Cosmi Historia

Robert Fludd • 1617-1621 • Latin Hermetic

Fludd's encyclopedic cosmology illustrated with extraordinary full-page engravings mapping the correspondence between macrocosm and microcosm. The most visually ambitious work of Renaissance Hermetic philosophy.

Early Modern

Aurora

Jacob Boehme • 1612 • German Theological

Boehme's first visionary work, describing creation as a process of divine self-manifestation through opposing principles. Written by a cobbler with no formal training, it inaugurated a theosophical tradition that influenced German Idealism and Western esotericism.
